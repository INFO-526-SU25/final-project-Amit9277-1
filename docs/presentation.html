Analytical Visualization on the History of Himalyan Expeditions

INFO 526 - Summer 2024 - Final Project

Cat Xia and Amit Chawla

Introduction

This project explores intriguing patterns and insights into mountaineering expeditions in the Nepal Himalaya.

  • By analyzing an extensive dataset of Himalayan climbs, the project seeks to uncover relationships between the choices climbers make: -

    — such as their selected routes and expedition agencies

    — and their chances of success or risk of fatalities.

  • The analysis particularly aims to shed light on how these factors vary across different nations and time periods, offering a deeper understanding of what influences expedition outcomes in one of the world’s most challenging environments.

The Himalayan Database

  • Contains the History of Himalayan Mountaineering Expeditions from Tidytuesday, taken from the archive by Elizabeth Hawley from the Himalayan Dataset.

  • Details peaks, expeditions, expedition related aspects, outcomes, and geographic data

  • Analysis uses two tidy tibbles: peaks and expeditions

  • Note: Expeditions dataset filtered to years 2020–2024 for manageable file size

Question 1

Question 1:

Are certain routes favored by expeditions from particular nations, and do they have disparate success rates?

Introduction:

  • The idea is to find out if there is any affect on success or failure based on the chosen route.

  • Analysis truncates the low volume attempts to avoid outlines.

Approach:

  • First I have calibrated the general senses of top 4 peaks attempted by nations, to identify the general success rate of the peak.

  • Next, visualized each peak and success percentage of popular chosen routes by nation, in a bubble chart

  • To finalize, the visualization and derive interpretation. I combined all 4 charts in one.

General Senses

The general senses shows that 3 out of top 4 peaks attempted for expedition shows fairly positive results around 90% success rate.

  • Now we will see what role does choice of route plays here….

Plot Analysis

Observations

  • Dominant Route Preference: A significant majority of attempts concentrated on a single, well-established route for this peak.
  • Marginal Alternate Success: While other routes were attempted, their success rates or popularity were considerably lower, highlighting a clear preference for the primary path.
  • Strategic Path-Peak Selection: Top-performing nations consistently favored a specific path-peak combination, indicating that route selection is a critical determinant of expedition success.
  • Inherent Route Bias: The observed pattern reflects a historical trend where a particular route, proven over years, emerged as the most reliable for success by 2020-2024 dataset.

Key Limitations

  • Data Quality: Initial dataset required extensive cleanup due to inconsistencies (e.g., consolidated route information in one column instead of four).

  • Data Sparsity: High variable count relative to entries limited conclusive findings.

Future Directions

  • In-depth Dataset Exploration: Our team intends to expand our analysis by leveraging the more comprehensive data available in the original dataset’s second edition, accessible at: https://www.himalayandatabase.com/hbn2019.html This will enable a deeper investigation into the insights observed in the current smaller dataset.

Question 2

Question 2:

Do certain Agencies have a higher number of member/personal deaths than others with respect to season/date?

Introduction:

  • Do Himalayan expidition fatality corellate with any Agencies? If so, further research could look into the safety guidelines of the more fatal agencies and why they occur.

  • Are fatalities more common in certain seasons?

Approach:

  • First, look at number of fatal expeditions by agency

  • Next, visualize and compare agencies by percent fatality and season.

  • Lastly, see if the percent misrepresents the raw number.

Observations

  • Seven Summit Trekks had the most expeditions that had at least one fatality but often were not the most fatal per individual treks, and had low percentage of fatalities for each expedition.
  • Spring was significantly more deadly than Autumn
  • 2022 and 2023 had more deaths than 2021 and 2024
  • Slightly more Hiker fatality than Staff fatality

Limitations

  • Some expeditions had very low number of total participants and some had very high number of participants. This made some percentages hard to compare (ie 100% fatality was due to only 1 person on the expedition).
  • Tried to address this with raw values, but may be another approach.

Future Directions

  • Wider Societal Analysis: Our team intends to look at wider correlations or causal relationships regarding fatality during Himalayan expeditions, such as:

    • Are certain agencies owned by the same parent corporations? Are they tied to increased chance of fatalities?

    • What weather conditions during Spring do climbers face and have them been changing due to climate change and are there any commonalities in weather conditions during the more deadly years.

    • Is there any correlation between larger or smaller expedition groups in their likelihood of fatality?
